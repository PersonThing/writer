The phrase has come to stand for how being pressured to uphold masculine values of valour, courage, strength, responsibility, paying the bill, etc. eventually hurt men than help them by holding them to an impossible standard and setting them up to fail. Meaning, ‘mard ko dard hota hai’ (men do feel pain) and just like strength is not just a ‘male’ value, ‘pain’ is not just a woman’s burden.

![](/portfolio/images/editorial--death-in-the-gunj--02.png)

Portraying this pain in beautiful helplessness by Vikrant Massey as Shutu in ‘A Death in the Gunj’. His given name is Shyamal Chaterji and the movie can be seen as the chasm he’s trying to bridge between these two identities; trying to resist being infantilised but still denied entry into the adult circle.

He derives his power from being the intellectual (as most of us that lack in the brawn department do) and has just failed his final exams, a secret that he carries with himself. His father has just passed away but we don’t see him mourn because ‘ladke nahi rote’ (boys don’t cry). He acts out in a different manner, wearing his dead father’s sweater, an oversized jumper on a young boy’s shoulders.

![](/portfolio/images/editorial--death-in-the-gunj--03.png)

His antithesis is Vikram, the obnoxious loud male bully played so well by Ranvir Shorey that, by the end of the film, you’re rooting for the doom that has been impending throughout the movie to crash on him.

Shutu tries so hard to be what he thinks is manly and, replace Vikram; on his bike, with his woman, even on the seat on the picnic table. He is trying to play at being an adult while others around still treat him like a child. Asking him to pass this, fetch that — throughout the film he is seen making drinks for others and not ever having one himself.

![](/portfolio/images/editorial--death-in-the-gunj--04.png)

Contrary to that, there is an impatience expressed at his not being mature; not taking the role of the man in his family now that his father is no more, or even not even being adept a driving — the symptoms of a rite of passage to maleness.

And it kills him; trying to be this hyper masculine character that might be disliked in the movie but readily exists around us — in our everyday lives, our homes and offices. Because he is rejected as a lover by Mimi (Kalki Koechlin) and mocked as fearful by Vikram, his inner torment plays out at night; banging and whimpering at the wall in his sleep. This student who has always done well cannot come to terms with these sexist qualifications of growing up. The things he is good at: empathy, nurture, EQ, literature; are feminine values, like his feminine looks (as said by Mimi ‘you’re so pretty you could be a girl’) are denied recognition like the chess game he’d almost won were it not for the last minute interruption.

![](/portfolio/images/editorial--death-in-the-gunj--05.png)

The father, played deftly by Om Puri, is a mild irritant to the son and almost put out to pasture because he is not the man of the house anymore. To seek his relevance again he decides to pass on the bastion of supposed maleness to Shutu by showing him how to handle a gun, at the same time restricting his access because ‘yeh bacchon ki cheez nahi hai’ (this is not for kids).

In this film, Konkana Sen Sharma manages to hold up a mirror to our society that is teaching our boys everyday that it is more important to be loud than right, boorish than empathetic and dominating rather than welcoming. Strength has come to mean how much you can take rather than how much you can give and this strength is strangling our men into putting up a false show of bravado that they themselves often don’t agree with.
