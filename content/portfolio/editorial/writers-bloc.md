## The Side Effects

After having written a novel on relationships, suddenly you are the crowned agony aunt. Every woman has her own problems. Her expert opinion would be “women spend more time analysing what men think, than men do thinking”. So the advice is to just chill.

Then there’s the dentists’ chair. They have a set procedure of asking you the same question that they ask all patients to make them comfortable. One of them is, ‘so what do you do?’, ‘I’m an author’, ‘really, which book?’, ‘_Almost Single_’, and while wielding the monstrous, demonic drill machine midway comes the query, ‘are you Aisha?’ There are also those who ask, ‘how is married life’ (Aisha being the protagonist of the book, who finds her prince charming).

Ok, so we understand the book is ‘inspired’ from real life, nonetheless and the real life bits would be… when Misha’s cheap date throws up on her shoes!! Confirmed by the author, much to real life Misha’s dismay, shit happens. We concur that dating should be declared a sport and one should have strategy experts who could double up as relationship gurus/authors by day, because in this labyrinth the Minotaur may also get lost.

## The Changeling

There are so many feathers in this cap; it’s practically a hen house! She started off as a librarian, even before the nerd glasses became cool. King of the bibliotheque moved on to become a teppanyaki chef (we hear the teppanyaki bit is an embellishment, writers jeez) where she claims to have sent eggs projectile through the kitchen, the self proclaimed eggzpert can whip up eggzactly what you want if it has to do with, yes, eggs.

Then there was the stint at the Laundromat — yes you read that right! Moving on to the shortest one yet at a drive through lasting a humongous five hours! You can’t really blame her if the southern Scarlett O’Hara fuses with an Indian accent — but the brave one walked away from that with dignity.

In retrospect, we realise that she’d been following the set pattern that has been established by so many authors. The creative people have a reputation for inconsistency, short attention span, the longest running affair with boredom and other romantic illusions. It was in between time zones, travels and jobs that the book is born. Thank God for change.

## The Immaculate Conception

It is not like one fine day she woke up and decided to write a book. If only. This Jane Bond of writing (yeah, she can be slick) has been a closet author since the age of nine. Taking her then finished manuscript to the most esteemed literate at that age, the English teacher, to directing plays among friends — she writes for herself, about mocking the things that annoy her, sharpening her wit with each rib tickling blow. Writing, she says, was the easy part. When she writes, her characters are alive in her head. How do I know? Because she says, the book is also getting printed in French, Aisha would be so happy! As she writes from multiple points of view, she becomes them — makes me wonder about the sanity of authors, do they all have MPD’s? But that’s another issue.

There were the nightmares, the ‘will they-won’t they’ anxiety ridden period, automated rejections, till one beautiful morning that brought the phone call from Harper Collins. She now has her reputation established but misses the freedom of writing. The pressures of catering to a select audience, of dissociating with the first book in terms of a creative process is impossible.

## The Morning After

Being a celebrity author has its perks, but does she resent the term chick lit? Well, from the horse’s mouth, “I don’t react to it. It’s just negative energy. There is so much positive that has come out of the book.” For her to complain that why are they not calling it ‘wit-lit’ or ‘women’s commercial fiction’ or something equally pretentious, is a waste. Cancer patients, mothers, daughters, colleagues have shared it.

It takes smarts to write a book and to treat it in an honest, funny way. When was the last time a comedy won a Booker or an Oscar? Jim Carrey draws in the crowds but doesn’t walk away with the statues. So you take what you get. Nothing is complete in itself. With wisdom beyond her years she tips me ‘don’t try too hard to conform, or to rebel’. She does not feel the need to validate the significance of her books by having someone mull over them or write something angst-y or full of despair. The story determines the narrative, which in this case was a certain style and it worked.

## Mightier than the Sword

The book is very _Sex and the City_, because it’s about female bonding, just sitting together, talking and sharing lives, which recalls a very _Pride & Prejudice_ style of writing minus the Dom Perignon, so by that definition have we changed/evolved at all? Pat comes the reply, ‘no’. And not only haven’t we changed over time but also over geography. The book is selling in the U.S. and France, and it wouldn’t be considered liberal by European standards but you have women in these places buying it and reading it. The experience migrates beyond time and geographical boundaries.

But by that premise, is that all that women do… gossip, mostly about men? And she enlightens me again that it’s not all that we do, but it’s definitely an aspect of what we do. The whole post-feminist phase has been about going back to the things that we feel are important. It’s about not looking at men antagonistically and realising that we need to make it work together, and it’s reflective in literature. There are days when you wonder if you’ll wake up at the age of 45 and not be married, there are days when you feel horrible and desperate about not having a man in your life, and you needn’t feel apologetic about it. These moments of weakness, she says, are liberating.

## Knight in Shining Armour

Is there a need for a prince charming… well, there is one in the book, but that is fiction. Romantic love is a luxury; there are many other salient points in the book. Aisha thinks that she wants to get married, but when the perfect suitor comes her way, she doesn’t know how to react. We have been programmed to think we want certain things (by our mothers, the marriage bureau council, the collectors of _kundalis_ and family _pundits_), and it’s tougher for single women in their thirties and beyond because it doesn’t add to their stud status.

An antithesis, playfully quotable, a line a minute; spilling _gyan_ that has gathered over the years (barely any years) condensed in a line. You’d naturally expect an author to be good with words but coupled with such honesty, it disarms. She is health conscious ‘the choices that you make today are the diseases that you’ll live with tomorrow’, steers clear of tags and stereotyping, comfortable in her skin, appreciates Delhi for the attitude of the people (because we are just hanging out all the time), likes the humour in the place, detests the extra gloss in her ‘author’ picture. So when you want to curl up in your blanket with a feel-good book, you know exactly what to pick up.

## Fast & Furious

**My favourite phrase in the book is ‘umbilical cord whiplash’, what’s yours?**
Skinny dipping in sleaze!!

**What’s the interview count since the book launch?**
Early three digit numbers.

**If you had to give the Booker prize, you’d give it to…**
Aravind Adiga, I loved the book!

**What are you reading right now?**
_Falling off the Edge_ by Alex Perry.

**The perfect Sunday morning is…**
Packed with three hours of _CSI_.

**Men are from Mars; Women are from Venus. Nic and Ric (the gay couple in her book) are from…**
Heaven, she adds all angelic.

**Mother-in-laws never…**
Let you down!

**If you were a song?**
_I’m like a bird_ by Nelly Furtado.

**If _Almost Single_ were a dish?**
It’d be something sinful like hot chocolate fudge or bull’s eye (a dessert at Machan, Taj Mahal hotel).

**And the movie would be called?**
_Lag-bhag Single_ (laughs hysterically).

**A modern day curse?**
Dieting.

**Lesser known fact about Advaita?**
Used to be a Goth Chick.

**Are you single?**
ALMOST.

**How do you hunt?**
He needs to be very well spoken and compassionate.

**Any single girl _gyan_?**
Always keep the mystery.
