# Masculinity, One Closet at a Time

The curtains draw, a lithe figure on Pointe shoes trots out. It is the adagio of the Swan Lake and Yekatarina Verbosovich is a vision of skill and a product of training. But this ballerina is more Swan Kween than Swan Queen.

Winner of the best male ballet dancer at the National Dance Awards held in the UK this year in February, Chase Johnsey is the first to win this award for a drag performance. That he was a dance prodigy since the age of 14 has been amply clear, but for vanguards of ballet tradition to recognise his talent even as he mixes ballet and drag is exceptional.

The world shrinks as it expands. The more open we get to differences, the more we police them. Today, thankfully there is more than one way of being a man, gender roles increasingly have stopped being prescriptive and yet, hate crimes against those perceived as 'different' are on the rise.

These are men who don women's clothing, in everyday life, on the stage, for a job or even for personal pleasure. In a world of male privilege, how we treat those who give up this privilege even occasionally, is rather telling.

Tsohil Bhatia, Delhi-based performance artist, does it for the kink. “I really enjoyed drag because it made me feel slightly unsafe; that was exciting.” He's come to enjoy the male gaze, admittedly, because he does not experience it as a woman but a man in drag. “We feel so safe having you around,” say his female friends on a night out, “because no one is looking at us, everyone is looking at you.”

And yet there is no reason for the staring and the exception. Comedy on Indian television has made drag mainstream to a large extent. Men dressing as women, be it Shakespeare or Kapil Sharma, the tradition has lived through the ages.

But there is an essential difference, explains Kareem Khubchandani, asst prof. of performance studies at Tufts, and drag queen beyond compare as LaWhore Vagistan, “For most part, they ('straight' men doing 'drag' on YouTube) do it to mock femininity, but in order to mock it they must actually do it and show it off in public.”

Even if we look at the portrayal of drag in Kapil Sharma's show, women are not allowed to express desire without being mocked. The 'dadi' played by Ali Asgar can be rambunctious about it because we know it's a man eventually. The 'unwed bua' on the other hand, played by a woman, is figure of pity when she expresses desire. Even Kiku or Sunil Grover play young girls and portray them as cringe-inducing village idiots.

This is where Drag performances are different from straight men cross dressing on TV. You laugh at the material, the wit, the sass; not the female. “Cinema portrays guys playing women as comical. I don't portray that but show Mayamma as a woman”, says Alex Mathews.

Mathews is a Malayali boy from Hyderabad, based in Bangalore. And if you think that is confusing, this boy transforms into beautiful Mayamma, the drag avatar named after the Mother of Illusion. “Every time I am on stage I am serving illusion,” he says.

What makes the performance different is that, “…in my drag avatar I am imitating my mother, my grandmother, my relatives.” Mayamma is a Malayali woman who is proud of her roots, who sings Malayali songs, wears beautiful saris, has a strong regional accent that the theatre people wanted her to get rid of but she persisted anyway.

And this makes the essential difference between character and caricature. Kareem's LaWhore Vagistan (colossal pun aside) is a more campy fun character but nowhere does it descend into offense or come from a place of malice and, as Mathew says, “She's a real aunty.” The big difference between laughing at and laughing with someone separates mainstream TV from these alt sources.

Drag has been part of mythology, religion and local theatre so much that it is a part of the public conscience in India and yet it enjoys a love/hate relationship with the masses. We are happy to laugh at it from a distance, but if one of your own is doing it, there is trouble in paradise. Mathew's mother gave him her own sari and blouse for his first performance; thinking it's a one off thing. When it became regular it became a problem.

Arjun, in the Mahabharat, crossed over as the female dance teacher Brihannala. Even in present day, the town of Kollam in Kerala celebrates the festival of Chamayavilakku, where men, most of them straight, cross dress as women and offer prayers to be forgiven for the sin of man. Many of them shave their moustaches for the ceremony. And yet, take away the religious excuse and you could be shoved around in a Delhi club for doing the same thing, as Bhatia found out.

Rampant homophobia means there are more female allies that come to the rescue in these situations than men, but in the same breath Bhatia mentions, “even homosexuals are trans-phobic. Try putting an androgynous picture on Grindr, months will pass before you get a match.”

“Given how widespread this practice (of cross dressing) is, I think it opens up questions about the pleasure (straight) men take in performing in feminine ways, given the ubiquity of these kinds of performances,” Kareem puts it rather astutely, I would just like to simplify it one step further.

Why is it that a woman dressing as a man is considered fashionable, but when a man dresses as a woman, more often than not, he is the subject of ridicule? Why is this pleasure denied to men? Because gender is not a spectrum as we would like to believe, it is a hierarchy. A woman aspiring to male traits is natural, aspirational even, but a man, stepping down from his male privilege to try on woman's clothing – must be askew!?

Straightening this askew is Harish, the only notable hetero guy who has made Queen Harish, his drag avatar, the talk of the town of Jaisalmer, and indeed the world. Having performed with biggies such as DJ Cheb I Sabbah, and on India's Got Talent, his show has travelled the world over and made him one of the early pioneers in the drag scene in India.

Jaisalmer has long had dancers and folk musicians that perform for the large but floating tourist population. Caught somewhere in-between the nautch tradition of grand old India, the cross-dressing tradition seen often in western UP and a desire to be a dancer, Harish picked up the lehenga purely for financial reasons.

“I wanted to be a conventional guy, enjoy the 9-5 job, I dreamt of being a teacher, and come back to my family,” a personal tragedy left him wanting cash and he was in the right place for this profession to be lucrative. He is straight, has a wife and kids, and easily compartmentalises his professional and personal life – this is a job, which is a big part of who he is, but not anymore than being an architect or a lawyer impacts ones life.

In fact, everyone mentioned in this article has a flair for performance and would have ended up on the stage one way or the other – cross dressing, cis dressing or no dressing. “Dance was, is and will always be my hobby” Harish says, and if it has given him fame, repute, livelihood, why not?

I borrow from Judith Butler when I say 'gender is performative' – it is not a stable identity, and one repeats tiny insignificant actions everyday to belong to, or conform to, one category or the other. Someone who is cross dressing has broken free of a binary choice that society makes available to us, and therefore drag is naturally subversive. “In imitating gender drag implicitly reveals the imitative aspect of gender itself”, says Butler.

A man with long hair, a girl in pants, a salesman draping a sari onto himself to show it to the customer – these are condoned forms of drag, a man wearing lipstick is not (unless he is Riteish Deshmukh who must then quickly become overtly hirsute to show symptoms of masculinity).

But where does gender lie? Body, mind or clothes? Is it how you pee or what you see? Can perspective change gender? Or is that sexuality? Mathew puts it as simply as one can, “sexuality is who I am, drag is what I do.”

Harish turns into Queen and plays the character to the hilt. As Queen he is coquettish, ravishing and a performer to behold. Chase Johnsey says, “When I first got into drag, and most people agree with me, I saw my mother”. Alex takes forever to turn his 'manly' face to the soft version of a female's and yet there are those who don't go all the way. For Tsohil, drag is not a transformative but an additive experience; he is not trying to become a woman but man and woman at the same time, chest hair peeking out of low necklines et al.

“I have had very strong women in my life and this is a tribute to all of them”, explains Johnsey, and we are grateful to all these women, for raising sons that don't think wearing bangles or skirts is an insult.
